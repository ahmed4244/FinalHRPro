# The Software Engineer career ladder

The map for the next ten years. You start today with a **bachelor's degree**, on
the first rung as a **Software Engineer**. The ladder climbs one rung every five
years, so two promotions are in reach by year ten:

| Year | Rung | Title | The jump |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (now) | 0 | Software Engineer | you are here — a bachelor's, ~0 yrs |
| +5 | 1 | Senior Software Engineer | own your work end-to-end |
| +10 | 2 | Staff / Lead Software Engineer | move more than one team |

Rung 1 is the note [[senior-software-engineer]]; rung 2 is [[staff-software-engineer]].

The model is the **big-tech individual-contributor (IC) track** — the ladder used
at companies like Google (L3→L5→L6), Meta (E3→E5→E6) and Dropbox (IC1→IC3→IC5).
It is the most concrete, most widely recognised version of the software career,
which is why we chose it ([[../projects/profession-ladder/adr-002-model-the-ladder-on-the-big-tech-ic-track|ADR-002]]).

## The one line that explains every rung
Each promotion is a jump in **scope** — how much of the world your work touches.
Every published ladder tells the same story: **task → feature → system → org**
([[the-scope-ladder-task-to-org]]). A Software Engineer finishes tasks; a Senior
owns features and systems; a Staff engineer changes how several teams work.

## How a promotion is actually won
You don't get promoted for *time served* or for *being ready* — you get promoted
by **already operating at the next rung** long enough that the title just catches
up ([[promotion-is-operating-at-the-next-level]]). So the plan is never "wait five
years." It is: do the courses, earn the certs, and ship the projects that make you
look like the next rung *before* the review.
