# Lead / Principal Aerospace Engineer (the second promotion)

Rung 2 on the [[aerospace-engineering-career-ladder]], the target at **year ten**.
You move from owning a subsystem to **owning the system** — the architecture and
integration a whole program builds on — the chief-engineer track.

## The scope
You set the technical direction several teams build against, own system-level
tradeoffs, and carry the program through its milestones
([[the-scope-ladder-task-to-org]]). Gated on program-level impact, not tenure.

## What it takes
- **Technical:** system architecture and integration across subsystems; tie
  engineering decisions to mission requirements, cost, and safety; make the tradeoffs explicit.
- **Leadership:** technical authority across teams without direct reports; sponsor
  senior engineers; represent the discipline in program reviews and hiring.
- **Experience:** ~10–15+ years.

## Certifications
- **INCOSE CSEP** (Certified Systems Engineering Professional) — the systems-
  engineering standard for the lead track; **PMP** for program leadership.
- Depth in the governing standards: **DO-178C** (airborne software), **AS9100**, airworthiness.

## Courses & reading
- The **INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook**; advanced study in your domain
  (GNC / propulsion / structures) at the system level.

## Projects that prove it
- **Own the system architecture / integration** of a vehicle or major subsystem used across teams.
- **Lead a program through its major milestones** (PDR → CDR → qualification / flight).
- **Own a multi-year technical roadmap** for a program and drive it to adoption.
- **Grow senior engineers** and set the engineering standards for the org.

## How the decision is made
Sustained program-level impact ([[promotion-is-operating-at-the-next-level]]): a
system architecture teams build to, programs delivered through the gates, and
technical authority recognised across the org.
